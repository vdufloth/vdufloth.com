![Vinicius Dufloth](/images/vinis-photo-profile.jpg)

Partner & Head of Tech at [Cyberlog](https://www.cyberlog.net), a B2B logistics software company. P&L on one side, engineering and product on the other, and a constant negotiation between them. Brazil-based; work remotely.

What follows is the long version — how a trainee developer ended up running both halves, and what we have built since.

## The Journey

I joined [Cyberlog](https://www.cyberlog.net) — a logistics software company my father co-founded — as a trainee developer. After his death in an accident, I inherited a minority stake at a critical moment, given the central roles he held in the company.

Shortly after, when the company posted negative cash flow for the first time in its history, the partners called on me to take over financial management.

I pursued education and knowledge in management, and what could have been just a forced transition became an opportunity for restructuring. I established financial governance — income statements, cash flow, budgets — and led complex corporate processes: valuation, partner exits and entries, elimination of operational bottlenecks, and a reset of strategic direction (drawing on external consulting as input, not driver).

In the following years, as the company grew, I also took on leadership of the technology sector with the challenge of implementing more efficient work management for the development team and transforming our products to be fully prepared for the web and the cloud.

## Today

As Head of Finance and Technology, I lead an engineering team of 10, plus customer support and finance.

We have implemented agile methodologies with predictable deliveries, a hybrid and distributed work model, and structured people development (career plans, continuous feedback, evaluations). Engineering headcount has held steady over three years while seniority deepened, turnover dropped, and output rose.

Our stack: Java services, Postgres and Oracle, Android and React on the client, Node + TypeScript for newer services. We have rebuilt engineering practice end-to-end — DevOps, automated tests, CI/CD, cloud infrastructure, proactive monitoring — and moved every critical service off on-prem (self-hosted GitLab, CI/CD, customer-facing apps, monitoring). We redesigned the CyberlogWMS architecture and started building a web-native replacement for the legacy system. Within a year of changed practices, open issues fell from 200 (56 behind schedule) to 60 (none behind).

On the finance side, revenue has doubled since I took over the books. Across customer deployments, the platform has processed 8M+ orders and 150B+ units to date.

Outside Cyberlog, I've also done part-time tech-leadership and advisory work for an early-stage startup. The same patterns repeat in most early-stage companies I've seen since.
